Let’s pause on the workflow we made in the previous two sections ([Activity 1](https://digitalhealthhumanities.github.io/toolkit/lessons/projectmanagement/digitalworkflows/3) & [Activity 2](https://digitalhealthhumanities.github.io/toolkit/lessons/projectmanagement/digitalworkflows/4)). The workflow we outlined could be applied to many different projects in many different disciplines, so long as they drew on network graphs as part of their methods. 

For this lesson, we are going to start to learn how specific media can inform unique workflows, and to do this, we will look through research materials acquired in an online archive. We are looking to these archival materials because they require some reflection as to how best to process them for research purposes. We have to ask questions like: Is this object important to include in my workflow? How will I examine this object to pull out research insights? What tools can help make sense of this object? Is it actually important for the research question I am trying to answer? Working from archival objects helps us see how different materials can be integrated into a single workflow. 

<div class="backsplash">**Understanding Why this Matters:** Our research *workflows* can be constructed to answer different needs—the needs of our preferred method, the *affordances* of our research objects, our comfort with specific tools or methods, and/or our institutional or personal resources. They are customizable, to a point, because it can be more difficult to rework a workflow halfway through a years-long project. (Sometimes, we realize that we have been doing something incorrectly and have to decide if and how we can solve that problem mid-stream.)</div> 

Let’s look at three objects, drawn from the [No More Silence archival dataset](https://www.library.ucsf.edu/archives/aids/data-projects/). These objects are related to community activism at the height of the HIV/AID epidemic. The first is a letter from Nancy Pelosi regarding the production of a film project on AIDS activism. The second is a button that reads “THE ONLY BUSH WITH BALLS IS BARBARA CURE AIDS NOW". And the third is a collection of newspaper clippings related to HIV/AIDS activism. (.pdf versions of this file can be found in the project repository on GitHub).

These objects are quite different from one another. One is a single page letter, the other a material culture object, and the last is a 170-page document filled with different clippings, letters, and other materials. Can we make a workflow that looks at each of these materials with equal weight? How can we think about these materials in ways that make them legible for our computational methods? 

These questions lead us to think about workflow in conjunction with our research questions. If our questions link to discussions of material culture, maybe we want to include the Barbara Bush button and the newspaper clippings. If our questions are interested in linguistic patterns across a corpus, maybe we do not care about the button. 

We are going to sketch out a workflow for these objects in the next lesson. Before we do that, spend some time thinking about these three objects and how they might be used to answer unique kinds of research questions. 

<div class="backsplash"><strong>Free Writing Activity:</strong> On a scratch piece of paper write out answers to the following questions:<br>

For each object, what is the most interesting element to you to research?<br>

For each object, what research method (digital or analog) might you use to analyze it?<br>

Looking at all three objects, how might you analyze two or three of them in a single research project? </div>

How do the questions we asked at the end of the last lesson help us determine the best course of action for a research project? They help us reduce the number of possibilities we encounter in our research. For the humanities especially, anything can be a {% include term.html term="text" %} and anything that is a text can be read using a range of reading-centered analytical methods. For some students and early career scholars the breadth of potential research projects afforded by an archival collection can lead to overly large projects, or forms of decision paralysis. It is important to remember the adage regarding any thesis, dissertation, or book-scale project, that **the best kind of a project is a finished project**. Because of this, our attention to workflow stresses scaling down projects, and understanding the limitations of a method or tool ahead of time, before a student or researcher spends months or years trying to fix a problem late in the research project.

We give this preamble to stress a point that is echoed throughout this toolkit: **it is good to have a sense of what you want to do before you do it**. Focusing on research questions, prototyping early in the project, and reflecting on the time and energy you have help keep the project scaled down. The next activity makes assumptions about a project engaging with the No More Silence materials we examined in the last lesson. We are choosing these rigid frameworks because they show how defining elements early on can help you choose specific methods, tools, and approaches, deciding what technologies you might need to effectively complete your research projects.

For the next lesson, we will take a single hypothetical digital research project and begin to link these materials to its workflow. This activity will show how attending to affordances informs the structure of a project, but as you may have noticed, when assessing these materials, that proposed workflow will only work for the very narrow example used in that activity.
