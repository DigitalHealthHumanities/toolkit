Let’s look at the project {% include term.html term="workflow" %} of a non-academic industry—film production—to help understand what a what we mean when we use this term. Filmmakers have, over the course of the last century, developed a robust, flexible workflow for cinematic production. Broken into three parts—pre-production, production, and post-production—cinematic workflows follow a mostly linear trajectory. Pre-production refers to everything before going to set: the screenplay, the budget, the hiring of actors, the testing of technologies, previsualizations and storyboards, set design, and location scouting. Production refers to everything that happens while filming: framing of shots, the management of the crew, the actors acting, and any script changes that occur during the filming. Post-production refers to anything after the production has wrapped, from the editing of the image, to adding special effects, to working with sound, to doing re-shoots.

This workflow is successful for this industry because it places every phase and technology into a structured sequence, where the {% include term.html term="affordances" %} of each technology are taken into consideration in relation to the costs and challenges associated with film production. An important part of this workflows is how the labor involved in the early stages of a project usually is designed to help the later stages flow more smoothly: It is easier to set up a shot if you storyboarded it beforehand, and digital effects are cheaper to produce if you have shots that are designed assuming that effects will be added in post. 

{% include definition.html term="Affordances" definition="The term ‘affordance’ refers to the material and technological limitations of a specific tool, platform, or method. For example, one of the affordances of a simple technology like a ballpoint pen is its ability to write." %}  

Unfortunately for a lot of digital humanities projects we do not have a concrete and standardized workflow from which to build. Often digital humanities projects follow a “build the plane while flying the plane" mentality, where scholars address unforeseen issues as they arise. We do not have as many models for the work we do, and we often use media of all sorts, kluging it into shape for the project. **No two digital humanities projects are the same**. This means that we can sometimes borrow aspects of established workflows, but other times we need to map out the project ourselves. 

What we will do in this lesson is start to piece apart our academic workflows with an eye for how we employ digital tools at different stages to accomplish small transformations in our arguments. This lesson will start with a hypothetical workflow, drawn using a program like Gephi, and then provide guidance and activities to help develop a workflow for your own project through a prototyping phase. This lesson will teach you how to think about workflow, both in terms of your primary materials and in terms of the platforms you may use for your research.
