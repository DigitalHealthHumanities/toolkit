To start thinking about {% include term.html term="workflows" %}, we will do an activity sketching out the various tools and media which are used to produce a scholarly research output—a journal article, a book, a digital humanities project. We are doing this for two reasons. First, we want you to think about all of the different technologies you might use, but take for granted in your work, and second, we want to show that many times we are already thinking about how certain pieces of evidence, data, and argumentative material link together across different platforms. 

**For this activity will need a few scratch pieces of paper, or a digital tool that can be used to draw in, like Microsoft Whiteboard. You may need more than one piece of paper over each of the activities in this extended lesson, as we will be asking you to add things to your sketched workflow graph, and you may have to erase or delete as you work on this activity.**

For this specific activity, we will think about the production a journal article in a printed academic journal. This output will be our {% include term.html term="deliverable" %}. This term, ‘deliverable’ is a flexible, open-ended way of describing a research output that is presented or given to another researcher, grant institution, or journal. It is, most simply, something that concretely shows the outputs of your work.  

{% include definition.html term="Deliverable" definition=" Deliverables are concrete research or methodological outputs which are prepared to be shared with others, based on yours and your receiver’s expectations. These can be very polished—like a published book manuscript—or something that is a work in progress—like a draft you submit to a writing group. Deliverables are most important because they give us clear goals to work toward, and they help benchmark progress." %}	 

First, write out on a scratch piece of paper, what deliverables would be necessary for a submission to a print-based, peer-reviewed journal. (We will be using the answers we give in the accordion below for the rest of this lesson, but if you have more listed, try to think through how those might be fit into your workflow!) 

{% include spoiler.html title="Our Answers" answer="1. A Microsoft Word File (.docx).<br>2. Images formatted for publication." %} 

We want you to think about your word processor as a central element of your workflow. Often, even for very complex digital projects, we still need to *write* various elements either for publication, for inclusion in our digital tools, or to help ground our ideas. Because of this, we need to be cognizant of how our different programs, file types, and data can be input and exported out of our text editor.  

It is important to understand that a tool like a word processor cannot do everything. It has specific {% include term.html term="affordances" %} that make writing easier. Because of its design the tool is a poor choice to produce images or process data, and we will need to adopt other tools to address these limitations. 

For this hands-on activity, we will outline how two tools—an image editor and a citation manager—interface with our word processor in the production of our deliverables. How do these tools output information that is then placed within a text file?  

First, draw out (with some space in-between) your deliverables and the tools that will produce those deliverables. For your deliverables be sure to include: Final Submission (this will encompass both the written file, any images, and any data). For the tools include 1) a word processor, 2) a citation manager, and 3) an image editor. 

Then, draw a circle or box around each, and if you like you can use colors to demarcate the difference between tool and output. 

Finally, draw arrows showing how those platforms are used to output the needed deliverables.

Even with simple projects, we still have to wrangle multiple technologies and tools to prepare them for publication. We may see our citation managers, which hold hundreds or thousands of unique entries collected over years of work, as a simple tool that makes drafting easier, but in the process we may forget the frictions caused when converting a list of sources on Zotero to a document on Microsoft Word. **Oftentimes, we do not recognize that we are working between programs, and take those transformations for granted.**  

We highlight this to show that you may already be working between programs to help draft scholarship, and that you already have some experience managing the frictions caused when implementing multiple tools in the process of doing research. When we bring in a digital research tool and the other tools that make it possible to apply a specific method to our research, we are doing the same thing, but might have to wrangle more unique platforms, address problems in formatting, and figure out how to simply get a tool to function. 

This workflow example is intentionally simple, and most written projects involve more moments where materials are ingested by or transferred between digital tools. When you write block quotes from a novel, how do you bring that into your essay? When you reference an archival object, do you refer to a picture you took in the archive or a scan you accessed online? When you cite an archival object, do you just cite it, or do you try to incorporate the archival scan into the text? 

We chose a manuscript in this first example because most academics have experience developing and delivering a manuscript for a class, a journal, or a conference, and while it can be complex, it can also be largely lingual. We are not thinking about data—in the form of a massive tabular repository or a complex file that can only be read with a specific tool—but instead thinking about the bulk of academic work: the writing of words. But what happens to our workflows when we start thinking about large scale objects, the processing of data, and the ways we fit that data into our written outputs? 

When thinking about a workflow we may ask: Where are we getting our data? How are we cleaning that data? What platforms are we using? If we use archival material, how do we make it machine readable? Once we have the data cleaned, how do we plan on analyzing it? How do we present that data in your work? How do we bring it into the text file?  

For the next lesson, we will use Gephi as an example workflow to start thinking about workflow more granularly.
